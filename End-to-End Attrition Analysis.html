End-to-End Attrition Analysis: Leveraging EDA and SQL for Diagnostic Insights and Targeted Retention Strategy.

Project Goal and Overview

The primary goal of this project is to conduct a diagnostic analysis of the employee attrition problem using an HR dataset. The project leverages a dual approach: first, a preparatory Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to clean and engineer features, and second, a rigorous SQL-first methodology using SQLite3 database to calculate attrition rates and uncover the root causes of turnover. The final output provides actionable, data-backed insights for HR retention strategies.

Steps Taken and Expected Outcome

Phase Steps Taken Expected Outcome
Part 1: EDA & Preparation Data retrieval, Data preprossesing, Feature engg & EDA, Correlation analysis and Key insights. A cleaned, structured dataset ready for SQL ingestion, and initial hypotheses on attrition drivers.
Part 2: SQL Implementation Creating a SQLite3 DB, Calculating overall and segmented attrition rates (by Gender, Dept, Age, etc.), Deep-dive querying to explore specific drivers (e.g., high-earner turnover, impact of overtime). A fully documented analysis with clear, quantitative results and specific risk factor identification.
Communication Utilizing markdown cells for thought process and observations in part 2; commenting all code for readability. A transparent, reproducible, and easily reviewable final report.

Part 1: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) and Data Preparation

This initial phase focused on preparing the HR dataset for reliable SQL analysis.

Data Quality and Feature Engineering

The EDA confirmed the dataset was clean with minimal missing values and no duplicates. Key preparation steps included: The project is structured to deliver deep insights through three core phases:

  • Outlier Treatment: Income-related variables (like MonthlyIncome) were inspected and outliers were capped (clipped) to prevent them from skewing aggregated results in the SQL calculations.
  • Feature Creation: New categorical variables such as AgeGroup and IncomeBin were created to facilitate effective segmentation and grouping in the subsequent SQL queries.

Initial High-Level Findings from EDA

The initial visual and statistical exploration highlighted strong correlations for further SQL investigation:

  • High Attrition: Younger employees and those with short tenure were the highest flight risk.
  • Correlating Factors: Overtime, low Monthly Income, and low scores on Environment Satisfaction were strongly associated with attrition.

    q1, q3 = s.quantile([0.25,0.75])

    return ((s < q1 - k*iqr) | (s > q3 + k*iqr))

3. Deep Dive into Attrition Drivers (The "Why")

Targeted SQL exploration revealed specific factors driving the decision to leave:

A. Factors Driving Short-Tenure Attrition (<5 Years) For employees with less than 5 years at the company, OverTime and Specific Job Roles are the dominant drivers:

  • Sales Representatives who work OverTime face an alarming attrition rate of 73.68%.
  • Laboratory Technicians who work OverTime show a very high rate of 60.53%.
  • Actionable Insight: The combination of a high-stress role and mandated overtime is severely impacting the retention of new talent.

B. Attrition Among High-Earners While low income often drives turnover, the analysis confirmed that people with higher Monthly Income still leave. This is linked to non-monetary factors:

  • High-income employees who left show a notable concentration in Low Job Satisfaction ratings.
  • Actionable Insight: For this group, compensation is not the problem; the focus should shift to improving the work environment and addressing Job Satisfaction scores.

C. Attrition in the 50+ Age Group The higher attrition in the 50+ age band (compared to 40-50) is disproportionately driven by specific roles:

  • Research Scientists and Sales Executives are the most common job roles contributing to attrition in the 50+ cohort.
  • Actionable Insight: Retention efforts for older staff should be targeted at ensuring these senior roles have appropriate career development or succession planning opportunities to maintain engagement.

Final Conclusions and Recommendations

Key Results Summary

The combined analysis reveals a dual-problem:

  • High-Volume Turnover: Driven by young, short-tenured employees in roles with mandatory overtime.
  • High-Value Turnover: Driven by senior and high-earning employees who are dissatisfied with non-monetary factors (e.g., Job Satisfaction, Role Engagement).

Recommendations

Based on the quantitative and diagnostic findings, the following interventions are recommended:

  • Mandatory Overtime Review: Immediately review and limit OverTime for early-career roles (Sales Reps, Lab Techs) to reduce the critical turnover rates in the <5 year tenure group.
  • Compensation Equity: Review salary bands for low-income employees to ensure competitive pay, as low monthly income is a strong general predictor of attrition.
  • Engagement Programs: Conduct periodic engagement surveys focused on Job Satisfaction and Work-Life Balance to identify and fix issues for high- earning and senior staff.
  • Mentorship and Retention Incentives: Introduce robust mentorship programs and retention incentives specifically targeted at early-career employees to improve tenure and performance.
